A Brief History of Endurance

490 BC
The First Marathon
Greek soldier Pheidippides ran ~40 km from Marathon to Athens to announce the victory over the Persians — the origin of the modern marathon.
1896
Olympic Marathon
The marathon became an official event at the first modern Olympic Games in Athens. The distance was standardised at 42.195 km in 1921.
1968
The Cooper Test
Dr Kenneth Cooper developed his famous 12-minute run test for the US Air Force — still one of the most widely used measures of aerobic fitness worldwide.
1982
The Course Navette
Canadian researcher Luc Léger developed the progressive shuttle run test, ideal for group testing in schools — still used in PE classes globally.
2019
Sub-2 Hours
Eliud Kipchoge (Kenya) ran the marathon in 1:59:40 in Vienna, redefining the limits of human endurance. His VO₂max is estimated at ~84 ml/kg/min.

Section 02A / Challenge Yourself

ENERGY SYSTEMS

To move, your muscles need ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate) — the universal energy currency of cells. Your body has three systems to produce ATP, each suited to different intensities and durations.

0 – 10 seconds
⚡
ATP–PCr System
Immediate energy, no oxygen required. Uses stored ATP and phosphocreatine (PCr) for instant muscle contraction. Fastest but depletes in 8–15 seconds. Recovers fully in 3–5 minutes.

Examples: sprint starts, jumps, throws, powerful kicks.
Anaerobic Alactic
15 seconds – 2 minutes
🔥
Lactic Acid System
Anaerobic glycolysis breaks down glucose rapidly without oxygen. Produces lactic acid — the cause of the burning sensation during intense exercise. The anaerobic threshold is the intensity at which lactate accumulates faster than it can be cleared.

Examples: 400m, 800m, intense pressing in team sports.
Anaerobic Lactic
2+ minutes
🌿
Aerobic System
Uses oxygen to oxidise carbohydrates, fats and proteins in the mitochondria. Highly efficient and virtually unlimited in duration. At low intensity, fats are the main fuel. At higher intensity, carbohydrates dominate.

VO₂ max is the key indicator: the maximum volume of O₂ the body can absorb, transport and use per minute (ml/kg/min).
Aerobic Oxidative

Section 02D

TRAINING METHODS

There are four main methods to develop endurance. Each targets different energy systems and produces different physiological adaptations.

〰️
Continuous Training
Sustained effort at constant pace, 20+ min. Develops aerobic base. Zones 2–3. The "conversation pace" test.
⏱️
Interval Training
High-intensity efforts with INCOMPLETE recovery. Improves VO₂ max and anaerobic threshold. Highly adaptable.
🌲
Fartlek Training
Swedish for "speed play." Unstructured pace changes during a continuous run. Combines all zones naturally.
🔄
Circuit Training
6–12 exercise stations with minimal rest. Develops muscular endurance and cardiovascular fitness simultaneously.

Section 02E

TRAINING PRINCIPLES

Training smart is more important than training hard. These scientific principles ensure your effort produces real, safe and lasting improvements.

📈
1. Progressive Overload
Gradually increase the training load to force adaptation
▼

The body adapts to the stress you place on it. If you always do the same workout, you stop improving. To keep progressing, you must gradually increase volume (distance/time), intensity (% MHR) or frequency (sessions/week). The 10% rule: never increase weekly volume by more than 10% from the previous week.

🔁
2. Continuity
Training must be regular and sustained over time
▼

Fitness adaptations disappear when you stop training (detraining). Cardiovascular adaptations can be lost in just 2–4 weeks of inactivity. To maintain fitness: 2 sessions/week. To improve: 3–5 sessions/week. Consistency beats intensity.

🎯
3. Specificity
Train in a way that resembles how you want to perform
▼

The body improves in what it trains. A swimmer should mainly swim, not just run. Muscular adaptations are specific to the movement pattern. Cross-training is useful to prevent injury and add variety, but the bulk of training should mirror the target sport.

😴
4. Recovery (Supercompensation)
Rest is part of training — without it there is no improvement
▼

Improvement happens during recovery, not during training itself. The body repairs and exceeds its previous level — this is supercompensation. Sleep is the most powerful recovery tool: 8–9 hours per night for teenagers is essential for training to produce its effects.

👤
5. Individualisation
Every person is different — training must be tailored to you
▼

There is no universal training plan. The same session produces very different effects depending on fitness level, genetics, age, sex and lifestyle. This is why personalised HR zones matter — what is hard for one person is a warm-up for another.

Section 02F

FITNESS TESTS

To know whether your training is working, you need to measure your endurance objectively. These are the most widely used tests in physical education.

🏃
Cooper Test (12 minutes)
The most widely used endurance test in the world
▼

Created by Dr Kenneth Cooper in 1968. Goal: cover the greatest possible distance in 12 minutes. VO₂max estimate: (distance in metres − 504.9) ÷ 44.73

LevelBoys 16 yearsGirls 16 years
Very low<2,100 m<1,700 m
Low2,100–2,300 m1,700–2,000 m
Average2,300–2,600 m2,000–2,300 m
Good2,600–2,900 m2,300–2,600 m
Excellent>2,900 m>2,600 m
📻
Course Navette (Léger Test)
Progressive shuttle run — ideal for school groups
▼

Run between two lines 20m apart in time with progressive audio beeps. Each minute (level/palier) the pace increases. Stop when you fail to reach the line before the beep twice in a row. Can be done indoors with the whole class simultaneously.

⏱
6-Minute Run Test
Accessible version of the Cooper — great for class use
▼

Same protocol as Cooper but in 6 minutes. More manageable for PE sessions. Ideal as an initial test at the start of the unit and a final test at the end to measure real improvement.
