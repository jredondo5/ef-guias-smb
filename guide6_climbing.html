Section 02A · Theory Block

History & Olympic Disciplines

Sport climbing's competitive history began in the Soviet Union in 1947. The IFSC (International Federation of Sport Climbing) was founded in 2007, and climbing made its Olympic debut at Tokyo 2020.

🏅
Tokyo 2020
Climbing's Olympic debut. Alberto Gines Lopez (Spain) won the combined gold medal.
First Olympic gold
B
Bouldering
Short powerful problems up to 5m without a rope. Focus on strength, technique and problem-solving.
No rope needed
LD
Lead Climbing
Roped climbing up long routes. Climber clips protection as they ascend. Falls go below the last clip.
Advanced technique
SP
Speed Climbing
Two climbers race on a standardised 15m route. World record: under 5 seconds.
Pure explosive power

Section 02B · Theory Block

The French Grading System

The French sport climbing grading system (used throughout Europe and internationally) grades routes from 5a (beginner) to 9b+ (elite/Olympic level). Understanding grades helps climbers progress safely.

5a-5c
Beginner
Moderate angle, large holds, simple sequences. Appropriate for school climbing sessions.
Starting level
6a-6c
Intermediate
Steeper angles, smaller holds, more complex sequences requiring technique and strength.
School development
7a-7c
Advanced
Overhanging sections, technical moves, significant strength required. Club-level training.
Club athlete
8a+
Elite
World-class level. Only achievable with years of specialised training and exceptional natural talent.
Professional athlete

Section 02C · Theory Block

Safety & Equipment

Climbing is inherently risky. Strict adherence to safety protocols is non-negotiable. These rules apply to all climbing environments — indoor walls included.

🦺
Harness
Worn around the waist and thighs. Must be fitted correctly before every climb — check tie-in point.
Essential check
🪢
Dynamic Rope
Stretches under load to absorb the energy of a fall. 9.5-10.5mm for single rope lead climbing.
Energy absorption
🔗
Belay Device
Friction device (ATC, Grigri) used by the belayer to control the rope and arrest a falling climber.
Belayer's tool
👟
Climbing Shoes
Tight rubber-soled shoes maximising friction on small holds. Snug fit essential for footwork precision.
Technical footwear

Section 02D · Theory Block

Technical Footwork

Climbing efficiency comes from footwork, not arm strength. The best climbers climb with their legs — arms provide balance and movement, legs provide the power.

👁
Read the route
Before starting, identify the sequence of moves from the ground. Plan the feet first.
Cognitive climbing
👟
Edging
Using the inside or outside edge of the shoe on small holds. Precision placement before loading.
Precision technique
🔲
Smearing
Pressing the sole against the wall where there are no holds — relies entirely on rubber friction.
Friction technique
🎯
Foot placement
Place the foot precisely and commit to it before moving. Never shuffle or search for the foothold.
Golden rule

Section 02E · Theory Block

Handholds & Body Positioning

Understanding different types of handholds and how to position the body to use them efficiently is the foundation of technical climbing.

✊
Crimp
Small edge gripped with fingertips. Strongest grip but highest injury risk — avoid in beginners.
Technical grip
🖐
Sloper
Round hold with no positive edge — requires open-hand grip and body tension to use.
Body tension hold
👆
Pinch
Hold gripped between thumb and fingers — common on artificial walls.
Thumb technique
📐
Drop Knee
Body position technique where the inside knee is dropped to create a hip turn, increasing reach.
Body position

Section 02F · Theory Block

Physical Training for Climbing

Climbing requires a specific physical profile. Understanding the physical demands allows climbers to train more effectively and reduce injury risk.

💪
Finger Strength
The most specific physical quality for climbing. Developed through hangboard training for advanced climbers.
Limiting factor
🧘
Flexibility
Hip flexibility is critical for high footholds. Core flexibility improves body position on overhangs.
Range of motion
🏃
Anaerobic Power
Bouldering and hard redpoints require explosive power over short durations — high intensity.
Energy system
❤️
Aerobic Base
Longer routes require aerobic endurance. Strong aerobic base = faster recovery between boulder problems.
Endurance foundation

Section 03 · Train & Compete

TRAIN & COMPETE

Complete these challenges. Record your results and upload evidence to Classroom. Always prioritise safety.

01
Boulder Problem (Beginner Grade)
Technical

Attempt 3 bouldering problems at beginner level (5a-5c or equivalent wall colour). Focus exclusively on footwork — where you place your feet, not your hands.

02
Route Reading Exercise
Cognitive

Stand below a route and, without touching the wall, identify: the first 5 hand moves, the first 5 foot placements, and the hardest section. Then attempt the route.

03
Hangboard / Grip Strength Test
Strength

If a hangboard or edge is available: hang with both hands on an edge for as long as possible (arms straight). If not, use a door frame edge or bar. Record time for 3 attempts.

04
Safety Protocol Demonstration
Safety

With a partner, demonstrate: correct harness fitting (verbal walkthrough), belay device setup, verbal commands (Climbing!/Climb on!/Take!/Lower!). Swap roles.

05
Reflective Climbing Session
Evaluation

Complete a full climbing session at the wall. Before each route, spend 1 minute reading it. After each route, note what went as planned and what surprised you.
