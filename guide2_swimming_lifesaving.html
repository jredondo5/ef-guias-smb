Section 02A · Theory Block

Water Safety Rules

Water safety rules exist to prevent accidents before they happen. Most drownings are preventable with the right knowledge and behaviour.

🚫
No Diving in Unknown Water
Always check the depth before entering. Hidden rocks cause serious spinal injuries.
Spinal risk
👁
Never Swim Alone
Always swim with a buddy or in supervised areas. Drowning can happen in silence.
Buddy system
🌊
Rip Currents
A fast-moving channel of water. Do not fight it — swim parallel to shore to escape.
Coastal hazard
🏖
Swim Between Flags
On guarded beaches, always stay in the supervised zone between the red-yellow flags.
Supervised zone

Section 02B · Theory Block

Recognising a Drowning Victim

Contrary to popular belief, a drowning person rarely shouts for help. Recognising the signs early is critical to a successful rescue.

👁
Silent Struggle
A drowning person cannot call for help — they use all energy to keep their airway above water.
No shouting
📐
Vertical Position
Body vertical in water, head tilted back, mouth at water level — not swimming horizontally.
Instinctive drowning response
👀
Glassy Eyes
Unfocused or glassy stare, hair over face, unable to respond to questions.
Recognition sign
💪
Arms Pressing Down
Arms pushing down on the water surface to keep the mouth up — cannot wave for help.
Key visual cue

Section 02C · Theory Block

Lifesaving Strokes

Different swimming strokes are used in different rescue situations. Each has specific advantages for the rescuer.

🏊
Breaststroke
The primary lifesaving stroke. Head stays up to maintain visual contact with the victim.
Head-up technique
↔️
Sidestroke
Used to tow a victim. One arm supports the victim while the other propels the rescuer.
Towing stroke
⬆️
Backstroke Tow
For victims who must be kept face-up. Rescuer tows while maintaining victim\'s airway.
Airway open
⚡
Front Crawl
Fastest stroke — used to reach the victim quickly before transitioning to rescue stroke.
Speed only

Section 02D · Theory Block

The PAS Protocol

PAS (Prevent, Announce, Save) is the internationally recognised protocol for responding to aquatic emergencies.

P
PREVENT
Stop the emergency before it develops. Identify risks, warn others, call professional services first.
Prevention first
A
ANNOUNCE
Alert emergency services (112 in Spain). Give location, number of victims, and current condition clearly.
Call 112 immediately
S
SAVE
Throw a ring, extend a pole, use a rope. Enter the water only as a last resort — never first.
Non-entry rescue first
⚠️
Last Resort
Entering the water puts the rescuer at risk of drowning too. Always try non-entry rescue first.
Rescuer safety

Section 02E · Theory Block

Rescue Equipment

Knowing what equipment is available and how to use it correctly can be the difference between a successful rescue and a double drowning.

🔴
Rescue Buoy
A hard foam float used by lifeguards. Thrown to the victim or held while swimming to them.
Primary tool
🪢
Throw Rope
A bag containing rope thrown to the victim who holds on while being pulled to safety.
Non-entry rescue
🛟
Life Ring
A circular buoy thrown from poolside or boat. Victim holds on and is pulled in.
Poolside use
🏄
Rescue Board
A paddleboard-style board used in surf rescue to reach victims in rough water.
Surf rescue

Section 02F · Theory Block

First Aid: Recovery Position & CPR

Once the victim is out of the water, immediate first aid is critical. Knowing what to do in the first few minutes can save a life.

🫀
CPR Basics
30 chest compressions + 2 rescue breaths. Push hard and fast — 100-120 per minute.
30:2 ratio
↩️
Recovery Position
For a breathing victim — on their side to prevent choking on water or vomit.
Airway protection
📞
Call First
Before starting CPR on an adult, always call 112 or ask someone to call while you start compressions.
112 first
😮‍💨
Rescue Breaths
Tilt head back, lift chin, close nose, give 2 breaths lasting 1 second each.
Airway manoeuvre

Section 02G · Theory Block

Swimming Techniques — The Four Strokes

Each swimming stroke has a distinct technique, energy demand and practical use. In a lifesaving context, choosing the right stroke at the right moment can determine the outcome of a rescue.

Key Technique Points

🏊
Front Crawl
Alternating overhead arm pull with a flutter kick. Face submerged except to breathe. Most efficient over distance. In rescue: used only during the approach sprint — head must lift periodically to locate the victim.
Fastest stroke
🐸
Breaststroke (head-up)
Simultaneous arm pull and frog kick. In lifesaving the head stays elevated throughout to keep the victim in sight at all times. Slower than crawl but allows constant visual contact — the fundamental rescue approach stroke.
Primary rescue stroke
🔄
Backstroke
Alternating arm recovery over the water with a flutter kick, face up. Used when towing a victim who needs their airway kept above the surface. The rescuer holds the victim under the armpits from behind and kicks to shore.
Airway tow
↔️
Sidestroke
Body on one side, scissor kick and asymmetric arm pull. One arm stays free to hold the victim during the tow. Very low energy cost — ideal for longer rescues when fatigue is a factor. The most commonly used towing stroke.
Towing + endurance

Section 03 · Train & Compete

TRAIN & COMPETE

Complete these challenges in the pool or sports hall. Record your results and upload evidence to Classroom.

01
25m Timed Sprint
Speed

Swim 25m front crawl as fast as you can. Record your time for 3 attempts. What is your best time? What technique would you improve?

02
Head-Up Breaststroke
Lifesaving stroke

Swim 2 lengths using head-up breaststroke — keeping your head out of the water throughout. This is the primary lifesaving approach stroke. Focus on maintaining vision forward.

03
Throw Rope Accuracy
Equipment

From the pool edge, practise throwing a ring or rope to a partner standing at 3m, 5m and 8m. Record how many successful throws out of 5 at each distance.

04
Simulated Tow
Partner rescue

With a partner, practise towing them 10 metres using sidestroke or backstroke tow. Then swap roles. Focus on keeping the victim\'s face out of the water.

05
PAS Simulation
Group exercise

In groups of 4-5, simulate a complete rescue scenario using the PAS protocol. Assign roles (rescuer, victim, bystander, 112 caller). Debrief what worked and what would you improve.
